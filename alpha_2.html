Thesis: The Collapse of a Star to the Size of a Quark - A Druid Particle

By: Aryan Verma

Abstract

This thesis explores the possibility of a star, such as our Sun, collapsing to a size where it becomes a "Druid" particle, a hypothetical quark-sized entity. The collapse has profound implications on the formation of new universes or the development of black holes. By integrating General Relativity, Quantum Mechanics, and Cosmology, this study presents potential outcomes of such a collapse and their relevance to the cyclical nature of the cosmos.

Introduction

The final fate of a massive star is one of the most intriguing questions in astrophysics. Stars like our Sun undergo nuclear fusion, producing energy through the fusion of hydrogen into helium. However, as the star runs out of fuel, it eventually undergoes a collapse due to the gravitational forces acting upon it. The extreme conditions created during this collapse could lead to the formation of a singularity, which might either result in a black hole or, as hypothesized here, give rise to a new universe. The concept of a "Druid" particle, a particle with the size of a quark, is introduced to explore this collapse's ultimate implications.

Theoretical Background

This research relies on several established physical theories:

  • General Relativity: Describes the behavior of massive objects under gravity and the fabric of spacetime itself.
  • Quantum Mechanics: Governs the behavior of particles on a subatomic scale, including wave-particle duality and quantum fluctuations.
  • Cosmological Models: Includes the concepts of cosmic inflation, the Big Bang, and multiverse theory, which suggest the universe may go through cycles of expansion and contraction.

Additionally, the concept of a "Druid" particle is introduced as a hypothetical construct, where a collapsing star shrinks to a size comparable to the Planck length (~10^{-35} \, \text{m}), possibly triggering the creation of a new universe.

Mathematical Model of the Collapse

To understand the collapse of a star into a "Druid" particle, we use the following key equations:

  • Schwarzschild Radius: The radius r_s of a black hole formed by a collapsing star is given by:
    • r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}

    Where G is the gravitational constant, M is the mass of the collapsing star, and c is the speed of light.

  • Planck Mass: The mass m_p at which gravitational effects become comparable to quantum effects is:
    • m_p = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar c}{G}}

    Where \hbar is the reduced Planck constant.

  • Energy Produced During Collapse: The energy E released during a star's collapse can be approximated by the change in gravitational potential energy:
    • E = \frac{GM^2}{R}

    Where R is the radius of the star prior to collapse.

The equations above model the essential processes that govern the collapse and energy production. The mass of the star decreases as it collapses under its own gravity, and the potential energy stored within the star's gravitational field is released in the form of radiation and matter compression.

Energy Production in Stellar Collapse

The energy produced during the collapse of a star into a "Druid" particle can be modeled as an exponential increase. This increase corresponds to the release of vast amounts of energy, as the star's mass and volume diminish towards the Planck scale, releasing radiation and matter compression.

Entropy Change During Collapse

As the star compresses, the entropy increases, leading to a higher state of disorder. The collapse into a Druid particle likely results in a significant increase in entropy, as the matter inside the star becomes increasingly chaotic before it reaches the Planck density and size.

Density of the Druid Particle

As the collapsing star shrinks, its mass is compressed into an ever-decreasing volume. The density of the resulting Druid particle increases exponentially as the star's radius diminishes. This density would likely approach the Planck density as the star reaches the size of a quark.

Cosmological Implications: New Universes and Black Holes

The collapse of a star into a "Druid" particle might not only lead to a black hole but could also serve as the catalyst for the creation of a new universe. This hypothesis is inspired by the idea of the Big Bounce, where a universe collapses only to be reborn in another cycle of expansion. Such a concept is also supported by the multiverse theory, where multiple universes can exist, each with different initial conditions.

If a collapsing star produces a singularity that is fundamentally different from a traditional black hole, it could trigger the formation of a new space-time bubble, leading to a fresh cosmic birth.

Conclusion

The collapse of a star into a "Druid" particle presents a unique insight into the end stages of stellar evolution. By applying the principles of General Relativity, Quantum Mechanics, and Thermodynamics, we see how such a collapse could lead to the formation of black holes or even new universes, establishing an ongoing cosmic cycle. These concepts align with theories such as the Big Bounce and multiverse theories, suggesting that the death of one universe could lead to the birth of another, furthering the idea of a cyclical cosmic structure.
