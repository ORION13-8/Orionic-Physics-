Introduction to VNDM

The Verma Nuuk-Dark Matter Theory (VNDM) proposes that dark matter is composed of Nuuks, a new type of particle with unique properties. Unlike particles that interact through electromagnetic or strong forces, Nuuks interact exclusively through gravity. This makes them undetectable by conventional means but still exert a noticeable gravitational effect, which is why they are believed to make up a significant portion of the universe's dark matter.

Key Principles of VNDM

  1. Nuuks as Hypothetical Particles: Nuuks interact solely through gravitational force, making them undetectable by conventional methods.
  2. Creation and Distribution: Nuuks are produced during high-energy cosmic events such as black hole formations, magnetar activities, and quasars, dispersing across the universe.
  3. Gravitational Framework: The primary interaction of Nuuks is gravitational. They attract each other and form invisible frameworks that bind galaxies and other celestial objects together.
  4. Nuuks in Cosmic Voids: When Nuuks are found in vast empty pockets of space, they condense into dense dark matter, while they remain less dense around celestial bodies.

Implications of VNDM

VNDM explains the existence and properties of dark matter without requiring the introduction of new, unobservable forces beyond gravity. The theory proposes that the gravitational effects attributed to dark matter are a result of a dense network of Nuuks, forming an invisible mass that can only interact gravitationally.

The theory accounts for the clustering of dark matter in cosmic voids and its absence around celestial bodies. This gravitational framework stabilizes galaxies and large-scale structures in the universe, filling the gaps in current models of cosmology.
